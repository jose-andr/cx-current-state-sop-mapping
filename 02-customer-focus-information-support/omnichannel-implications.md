## Why this matters

Omnichannel service depends on accurate, consistent and reusable service knowledge.

If service information is duplicated, outdated, unclear or difficult to maintain, future channels may provide inconsistent responses.

This can lead to:

- customers receiving different answers in different channels
- staff relying on informal knowledge
- repeat contact and unnecessary escalation
- slow response to urgent issues
- duplicated content maintenance effort
- weak foundations for chatbot, web, CRM or staff knowledge reuse
- poor confidence in service information

## Omnichannel design themes

## 1. Source of truth

Future channels need to know which information is authoritative.

Key questions:

- Where does service information originate?
- Which system is the source of truth?
- Which systems are publishing locations?
- Who owns each information type?
- Who approves changes?
- How are conflicting versions resolved?

## 2. Reusable service knowledge

Service knowledge should be structured so it can be reused across staff tools, customer-facing channels and future automation.

Key questions:

- What information is repeated across tools?
- What information is copied manually?
- What information could become a reusable knowledge object?
- What information is staff-only?
- What information is customer-facing?
- What information is suitable for chatbot, web, CRM or scripts?

## 3. First contact resolution

Service information should help staff resolve customer enquiries at the first point of contact.

Key questions:

- What information do staff need to answer common enquiries?
- What information helps staff decide the next best action?
- What content gaps cause escalation?
- What content gaps cause repeat customer contact?
- What service entries are unclear or incomplete?
- What content quality standard is needed?

## 4. Urgent update workflow

Hot topics, campaigns, media issues and service disruptions may require rapid updates.

Key questions:

- How are urgent issues detected?
- Who confirms the message?
- Who approves the response?
- Which staff need to know?
- Which tools or channels need updating?
- How is the update retired after the issue passes?

## 5. Knowledge ownership

Reusable knowledge needs clear accountability.

Key questions:

- Who owns service rules?
- Who maintains customer service guidance?
- Who approves changes?
- Who reviews content over time?
- Who resolves disputes or conflicting guidance?
- Who retires outdated content?

## 6. Content lifecycle

Knowledge should have a lifecycle, not just a publishing moment.

Key questions:

- When is content created?
- When is content reviewed?
- When is content updated?
- When is content archived?
- What happens when content is no longer accurate?
- What happens when no owner is identified?

## 7. Human review thresholds

Not all service knowledge should be automatically published or reused.

Key questions:

- What information can be safely reused across channels?
- What information requires service owner approval?
- What information requires legal, privacy, communications or operational review?
- What information should be staff-only?
- What information should be customer-facing?
- What information should be time-limited?
