# Shared Governance Gaps

## Purpose

This file captures governance gaps that may appear across both current-state functions:

- Customer Data and Systems Support
- Customer Focus Information Support

The purpose is to identify where unclear ownership, decision rights, approval pathways, standards or escalation rules create operational work, service risk or future omnichannel constraints.

This file does not assume that governance is absent.

It identifies areas to validate.

## Governance themes

## 1. Ownership

Clear ownership is needed for both customer data and service knowledge.

Questions to validate:

- Who owns the customer record?
- Who owns customer data quality rules?
- Who owns service information?
- Who owns Service Directory entries?
- Who owns urgent customer response guidance?
- Who owns decisions when information conflicts?

Future implication:

A future CX operating model may need named owners for customer data, service knowledge, system support and content lifecycle responsibilities.

## 2. Decision rights

Decision rights clarify who can make or approve a change.

Questions to validate:

- Who can decide that two customer records are duplicates?
- Who can decide that customer data can be corrected?
- Who can decide that service content can be published?
- Who can decide that urgent guidance can be sent?
- Who can decide that old content can be retired?
- Who decides when an issue is outside the function’s scope?

Future implication:

Decision rights should be documented so staff do not depend on informal authority or individual relationships.

## 3. Standards

Standards make quality repeatable.

Questions to validate:

- What makes a customer record clean enough?
- What makes records likely duplicates?
- What data fields are critical?
- What makes content usable for first contact resolution?
- What makes a Service Directory entry complete?
- What makes urgent guidance clear and safe to use?
- What standards are documented today?
- What standards exist only in officer judgement?

Future implication:

Future data and knowledge governance should define practical, usable quality standards rather than only policy-level intent.

## 4. Approval pathways

Approval pathways protect accuracy and accountability.

Questions to validate:

- Which data changes need approval?
- Which content changes need approval?
- Which urgent messages need approval?
- Which updates can be handled directly?
- What happens when approval is delayed?
- What evidence of approval is retained?

Future implication:

Future workflows may need lightweight approval states, approver roles and escalation rules.

## 5. Escalation thresholds

Escalation thresholds prevent unresolved work and inappropriate ownership.

Questions to validate:

- When does a data issue become a system issue?
- When does a CRM support request become a technical issue?
- When does a content issue become a service owner issue?
- When does an urgent update become an incident or communications issue?
- When should a manager be involved?
- How is escalation recorded?

Future implication:

Future support model should classify and route work by issue type, risk and authority required.

## 6. Lifecycle management

Data and service knowledge both degrade without review.

Questions to validate:

- How are stale customer records identified?
- How are duplicate records monitored?
- How are outdated Service Directory entries identified?
- How are temporary messages retired?
- How are hot topic updates closed?
- How are review dates set?
- Who checks whether the review happened?

Future implication:

Future architecture should include lifecycle states, review dates, expiry dates and ownership prompts.
